Biography

I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Economics at the Universitat de Barcelona, on the 2026–2027 job market. My dissertation, Essays on Gender Inequality, Educational Choices, and Labor Market Pathways, is supervised by Profs. Lídia Farré and Andreu Arenas and examines how educational choices, institutions, and public policies shape inequality over the life cycle, with a particular focus on gender gaps in STEM, school-to-work transitions, and labor market outcomes. I draw on administrative and survey data, randomized field experiments, and quasi-experimental methods to conduct policy-relevant research in labor economics, education economics, gender inequality, and applied microeconometrics.

Alongside this research, I work to translate empirical findings into policy-relevant evidence for institutional and public-sector audiences — reflected in the fieldwork and research–practice partnerships described below — and I am seeking research and policy roles that put that evidence to direct use.

Research

with Andreu Arenas and Lídia Farré
July 2025 Working Paper

Abilities and Career Choices: Addressing the Gender Gap in STEM Fields

Women remain markedly underrepresented in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM), particularly in math-intensive fields. This paper investigates whether gender gaps in STEM enrollment arise from differences in students' ability profiles across domains, focusing on within-student comparative advantage in mathematics relative to reading. Using linked administrative records for students in Catalonia, Spain, we relate standardized math and reading test scores in grades 6 and 10 to subsequent enrollment in STEM-oriented upper-secondary tracks and university majors. We find that math comparative advantage accounts for about 60% of the female disadvantage in upper-secondary STEM enrollment, but only about 20% of the gender gap in university STEM major choice — a difference in explanatory power driven by the distribution of relative skills in the selected population, rather than a stronger behavioral mechanism.

Presented at the BRIDGE Economics of Education Workshop (KU Leuven) and the SEHO Annual Meeting, 2025.

July 2025 Working Paper

Educational Gains from School Electrification in Rural Areas

This paper evaluates the educational impact of the Luces para Aprender Program — an initiative of the Organización de Estados Iberoamericanos (OEI) that installed solar panels and internet connectivity in rural schools across 13 Latin American countries — using its rollout in Uruguay, alongside the educational gains from on-grid electricity connections, which offer a different level of quality and access to facilities. Using data on the electrification of all rural schools between 1992 and 2021 and a difference-in-differences design, we find that on-grid electricity led to a 3% increase in minimum attendance levels, entirely driven by the period when technological devices such as laptops and mobile phones became widely adopted, with no effect on completion rates. The increase in attendance appears to be driven by students' ability to charge and use these devices — a benefit provided exclusively by on-grid connections — and lasts only one year, likely because nearby households gained access to electricity soon after the school did. Solar panels, which provide only a single outlet, show no effect on attendance or completion.

Presented at the European Association of Labour Economists (EALE), 2024.

July 2025 Working Paper

Strategies to Increase the Take-up of Social Benefits: Evidence from a Field Experiment

We test strategies to increase take-up of a municipal support program among cuidacoches — deeply vulnerable, informal street workers in Montevideo who watch parked cars for tips — among whom fewer than half hold a work permit despite low compliance costs. In a field experiment, we randomly assign one-on-one assistance from a professional social worker to help workers complete the permit paperwork, and, within that group, randomly assign coverage of the documents' direct costs. Workers who receive one-on-one assistance are three times more likely to obtain the permit than the control group; those who also receive cost coverage are four times more likely. Providing information alone has no effect, and the intervention generates no spillover to take-up of other, non-targeted social programs.

December 2020 International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, Vol. 144

Tablets for Deeply Disadvantaged Older Adults: Challenges in Long-term Care Facilities

We evaluate a national one-tablet-per-elder program (Plan Ibirapitá) among deeply vulnerable, institutionalized older adults in a Uruguayan public geriatric hospital — patients with dementia, low educational attainment, and no prior use of technology. Using a randomized field experiment and a difference-in-differences design, we find no significant effects on cognitive deterioration, loneliness, or self-esteem, cautioning against techno-optimism in ICT policy for the most disadvantaged elderly.

Policy & Practice

My work sits at the intersection of research design and the institutional constraints that shape whether it can actually be implemented. Across roles in Latin America, the United States, and Europe, I have engaged directly with public institutions and practitioners to design, implement, and communicate policy-relevant evidence — including field implementation of randomized evaluations with vulnerable populations, a research–practice partnership with a large U.S. school district, and the design of a funded school-based RCT in Catalonia.

Universitat de Barcelona
