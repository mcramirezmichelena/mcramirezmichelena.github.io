Biography

My research examines how educational choices, institutions, and public policies shape inequality over the life cycle, with a particular focus on gender gaps in STEM, school-to-work transitions, and labor market outcomes. I use administrative and survey data, randomized field experiments, and quasi-experimental methods to conduct policy-relevant research in labor economics, education economics, gender inequality, and applied microeconometrics.

I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Economics at the Universitat de Barcelona (expected 09/2026), where my dissertation — Essays on Gender Inequality, Educational Choices, and Labor Market Pathways — is supervised by Profs. Lídia Farré and Andreu Arenas. Alongside this research, I work to translate empirical findings into policy-relevant evidence for institutional and public-sector audiences, and I am increasingly drawn to research and policy roles that put that evidence to direct use.

Research

Working papers and published work.

with Andreu Arenas and Lídia Farré
July 2025 Working Paper

Explaining the Gender Gap in STEM: Evidence from Administrative Records and an Experimental Intervention

Using linked administrative records for students in Catalonia, we show that gender differences in comparative advantage in math relative to reading account for roughly 60% of the gender gap in upper-secondary STEM enrollment, but only about 20% of the gap in university STEM major choice. The gap widens sharply among high-skill students, with women who have strong math preparation remaining markedly less likely than comparable men to major in STEM.

Presented at the BRIDGE Economics of Education Workshop (KU Leuven) and the SEHO Annual Meeting, 2025.

with Magdalena Blanco and Natalia D'Agosti
July 2025 Working Paper

Educational Gains from School Electrification in Rural Areas

Exploiting the staggered rollout of electricity access to rural schools in Uruguay (1992–2021), we find that on-grid electricity increased minimum school attendance by 3%, an effect driven by students' ability to charge devices and concentrated in schools connected after 2000. The effect fades within a year, and off-grid solar panels — which provide only a single outlet — show no effect on attendance or completion.

Presented at the European Association of Labour Economists (EALE), 2024.

July 2025 Working Paper

Strategies to Increase the Take-up of Social Benefits: Evidence from a Field Experiment

In a field experiment with informal street workers in Montevideo, one-on-one assistance from a social worker made recipients three times more likely to obtain a municipal work permit, and four times more likely when combined with covering the cost of required documents. Providing information alone had no effect on take-up.

December 2020 International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, Vol. 144

Tablets for Deeply Disadvantaged Older Adults: Challenges in Long-term Care Facilities

We evaluate a national one-tablet-per-elder program (Plan Ibirapitá) among deeply vulnerable, institutionalized older adults in a Uruguayan public geriatric hospital — patients with dementia, low educational attainment, and no prior use of technology. Using a randomized field experiment and a difference-in-differences design, we find no significant effects on cognitive deterioration, loneliness, or self-esteem, cautioning against techno-optimism in ICT policy for the most disadvantaged elderly.
